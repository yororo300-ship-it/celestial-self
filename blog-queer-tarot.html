Queer Tarot: Reading Cards Without the Heteronormative Script

8 min read

Open almost any tarot guidebook and you'll notice something within the first few pages: it assumes you're straight. The Empress is "your feminine energy." The Emperor is "the man in your life." The Lovers card means "a relationship between a man and a woman." The court cards are divided neatly into Kings (masculine) and Queens (feminine), as if gender were a coin with exactly two sides.

This isn't just lazy — it's a fundamental misreading of what tarot actually is. Tarot is a system of archetypes, not a system of gender roles. And once you strip away the heteronormative assumptions, it becomes a far more powerful tool — for everyone, but especially for queer, trans, and non-binary readers who have always had to translate the cards through someone else's lens.

This guide is for anyone who has ever drawn The Lovers and thought: "But what does this mean for my kind of love?"

The Court Cards Are Not Gendered

This is the single most important reframe in queer tarot. Kings and Queens are not men and women. They are energetic archetypes.

Queens represent receptive, inward, nurturing energy — the power of holding space, emotional intelligence, and influence through presence rather than force. This energy exists in everyone regardless of gender.

Kings represent directive, outward, decisive energy — the power of action, authority, and making things happen in the external world. Again, this lives in everyone.

Knights represent movement, pursuit, and the energy of going after something with single-minded focus.

Pages represent beginnings, curiosity, and the energy of learning something new.

When a Queen of Cups appears in your reading, it doesn't mean "a woman in your life who is emotional." It means: nurturing emotional intelligence is what this situation calls for. It could refer to you, your partner of any gender, a friend, a parent, or simply the energy you need to embody right now.

Reframe Traditional: "The Queen represents a woman; the King represents a man."

Inclusive: "The Queen represents receptive, nurturing power; the King represents directive, active power. Both exist in everyone."

The Lovers Card Is About Alignment, Not Anatomy

The Lovers is probably the most over-simplified card in the deck. It gets reduced to "you'll meet someone" or "your relationship is good" — and almost always with the implied image of a man and a woman.

But The Lovers, at its core, is about alignment. It asks: are your actions aligned with your deepest values? Is this relationship — romantic, platonic, professional, or with yourself — built on authenticity? Are you choosing what's true for you, even when it's hard?

For queer readers, this card often carries an additional, deeply personal layer. It can speak to the courage of living authentically — choosing to love who you love, even when the world makes that choice difficult. It can represent the moment of coming out, the decision to stop performing a version of yourself that doesn't fit, or the profound relief of finding a relationship where you can be fully seen.

The Lovers doesn't care about the genders involved. It cares about whether the love is real.

Reading Love Spreads Without "He" and "She"

Most love-focused tarot guides use gendered language throughout: "What does he think of you?" "What is she hiding?" This isn't just exclusionary — it's reductive even for straight readers, because it reduces complex humans to gender stereotypes.

A more powerful approach uses position-based reading rather than gender-based reading. Instead of asking "What does the man in this situation want?" try asking "What does Person A need?" or "What energy is being brought into this connection?" or simply "What does this relationship want to become?"

Some specific reframes for common love questions:

Instead of "Will I meet a boyfriend/girlfriend?" try "What kind of love is trying to find me?" This opens the reading to possibilities you might not have consciously considered — including the possibility that the love you need right now is self-love, community love, or a form of connection you haven't yet imagined.

Instead of "Is he/she the one?" try "Does this connection align with who I'm becoming?" This shifts the question from fate to agency. It acknowledges that you are an evolving person, and the right relationship is the one that supports that evolution — regardless of who the other person is.

Instead of "What's the future of this relationship?" try "What does this relationship need right now?" This is more actionable and less anxiety-inducing. It focuses on what you can actually do rather than passively waiting for the cards to predict an outcome.

Cards That Resonate Differently for Queer Readers

Several cards in the Major Arcana take on additional layers of meaning when read through a queer lens. These aren't alternative interpretations — they're expansions of the traditional meanings that have always been present but rarely acknowledged.

The Fool — The leap into the unknown. For many queer people, this card resonates with the moment of coming out or transitioning: stepping off the cliff of social expectation and trusting that authenticity will catch you.

The Tower — The destruction of false structures. When what you've built is based on performing someone else's version of your life, The Tower isn't a disaster — it's a liberation. The structure that's crumbling was never yours to begin with.

The Star — Hope and healing after destruction. The card that appears after The Tower. For queer readers who have endured rejection, discrimination, or the pain of hiding, The Star is the quiet promise: you will heal, and you will shine — not despite who you are, but because of it.

The World — Wholeness and integration. The final card of the Major Arcana. It represents the integration of all parts of yourself into a complete, authentic whole. For anyone who has spent years fragmenting themselves to fit into boxes, The World is the card of finally, fully arriving.

Choosing or Creating an Inclusive Deck

The traditional Rider-Waite-Smith deck, created in 1909, reflects the gender norms of its era. While its symbolism remains powerful, many queer readers find that decks with more diverse imagery create a stronger personal connection.

Several modern decks are designed with inclusivity as a core principle — featuring diverse body types, skin tones, gender expressions, and relationship configurations. Some remove gendered court card titles entirely, replacing King/Queen with terms like "Visionary/Guardian" or "Fire/Water."

But here's the thing: the deck matters less than the reader. You can read a traditional deck queerly by simply refusing to accept gendered interpretations at face value. The cards are symbols. Symbols mean what you bring to them. Your lived experience as a queer person is not a filter that distorts the reading — it's a lens that enriches it.

A Note on "Tradition"

Some people argue that reading tarot "correctly" means adhering to traditional, often heteronormative interpretations. This argument has a problem: tarot has been reinterpreted and reimagined continuously for over 500 years. There is no single "correct" tradition. Every generation has adapted the cards to reflect its own understanding of human experience.

Reading tarot in a way that honors queer experience isn't a departure from tradition. It's a continuation of it. The cards have always belonged to whoever picks them up with genuine curiosity and honest self-reflection.
